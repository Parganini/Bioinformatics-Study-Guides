## Set 1 – Questions

1. Which of the following best defines a synapomorphy?
   A. A unique trait in a single taxon
   B. An ancestral trait shared by multiple taxa
   C. A shared derived trait inherited from the most recent common ancestor
   D. A trait that arose independently by convergence

2. An autapomorphy is:
   A. A shared derived trait among taxa
   B. A unique derived trait in one lineage
   C. An ancestral trait shared by a clade
   D. A hypothetical ancestral character

3. A symplesiomorphy refers to:
   A. A shared ancestral trait that does *not* define a specific clade
   B. A shared derived trait that defines a clade
   C. A unique derived trait in one taxon
   D. A trait resulting from convergent evolution

4. A monophyletic group must include:
   A. Its most recent common ancestor and *all* of its descendants
   B. Its ancestor but only some descendants
   C. Taxa from separate ancestors with similar traits
   D. Any set of taxa sharing a character state

5. Polyphyletic groups are characterized by:
   A. Including the common ancestor and all its descendants
   B. Including taxa that lack their most recent common ancestor
   C. Including only one lineage
   D. Always being fully resolved in a cladogram

6. Paraphyletic groups exclude:
   A. No descendants—include all lineages
   B. Some descendants of their most recent common ancestor
   C. All taxa outside the focal clade
   D. Only outgroup taxa

7. In a rooted phylogenetic tree, the root represents:
   A. The most recent common ancestor of all tips
   B. A terminal (leaf) taxon
   C. A hypothetical midpoint of a branch
   D. The fastest‐evolving lineage

8. What is the primary purpose of an outgroup in phylogenetic analysis?
   A. To estimate branch‐lengths in substitutions
   B. To provide a calibration for molecular clock analyses
   C. To root the tree and polarize character changes
   D. To define operational taxonomic units (OTUs)

9. Ingroup taxa are the:
   A. Lineages assumed outside the focal clade
   B. Taxa used to root the tree
   C. Focus of the phylogenetic analysis
   D. Hypothetical ancestors

10. Which diagram only shows branching order (topology) without meaningful branch lengths?
    A. Phylogram
    B. Chronogram
    C. Cladogram
    D. Distance matrix

11. In a phylogram, branch lengths typically represent:
    A. Time since divergence
    B. Amount of character change (e.g., substitutions)
    C. Number of taxa in each clade
    D. Bootstrap support values

12. In a chronogram (timetree), branch lengths indicate:
    A. Number of nucleotide changes
    B. Relative divergence times
    C. Bootstrap support
    D. Topological distance

13. The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution (Kimura) posits that:
    A. Natural selection drives most molecular changes
    B. Most changes are neutral or nearly neutral and fixed by drift
    C. Mutation rates vary unpredictably across genes
    D. Molecular clocks never deviate

14. The molecular clock hypothesis suggests that:
    A. Substitutions accumulate at a roughly constant rate over time
    B. Evolution proceeds in rapid bursts only
    C. All sites in a gene evolve at the same rate
    D. Clock‐like behavior holds only for morphological traits

15. Orthologous genes are those that:
    A. Arise by gene duplication within a species
    B. Diverge by speciation and retain the same function
    C. Perform similar functions but have different ancestry
    D. Are defined by reciprocal best BLAST hits

16. Paralogous genes originate from:
    A. Speciation events
    B. Gene duplication events
    C. Convergent evolution
    D. Horizontal gene transfer

17. Reciprocal Best BLAST Hit (RBH) infers orthology by:
    A. Phylogenetic reconciliation of gene trees
    B. Identifying bidirectional top BLAST matches between two genomes
    C. Clustering all‐vs‐all sequences into orthogroups
    D. Comparing gene synteny exclusively

18. The Needleman–Wunsch algorithm is used for:
    A. Global (end‐to‐end) pairwise alignment
    B. Local alignment
    C. Multiple sequence alignment
    D. Neighbor‐Joining tree estimation

19. The Smith–Waterman algorithm performs:
    A. Global alignment
    B. Local alignment of high‐scoring regions
    C. Construction of guide trees
    D. Neighbor-Joining tree estimation

20. An affine gap penalty scoring scheme is:
    A. c = -d * g (linear cost)
    B. No gap cost
    C. c = -d - (g - 1)e (gap open d, extension e)
    D. c = -d * g (constant cost)

21. UPGMA builds trees under the assumption of:
    A. A strict molecular clock (ultrametric distances)
    B. No molecular clock
    C. Minimum‐evolution criterion
    D. Unrooted output

22. Neighbor‐Joining differs from UPGMA by:
    A. Requiring ultrametric data
    B. Producing rooted trees
    C. Not assuming equal rates (no molecular clock)
    D. Using bootstrapping in tree building

23. Maximum Parsimony seeks the tree that:
    A. Maximizes likelihood under a model
    B. Minimizes total number of character changes
    C. Balances branch lengths equally
    D. Optimizes posterior probability

24. Jukes–Cantor (JC69) nucleotide model assumes:
    A. Different rates for transitions vs. transversions
    B. Equal base frequencies and equal substitution rates
    C. Variable site‐rate heterogeneity
    D. Stationarity only for amino acids

25. Kimura 2‐Parameter (K80) distinguishes between:
    A. Codon and amino acid changes
    B. Transitions and transversions
    C. Gamma‐distributed rates
    D. Local vs. global alignment

26. Felsenstein 1981 (F81) extends JC69 by:
    A. Adding a transition/transversion bias
    B. Allowing unequal equilibrium base frequencies
    C. Modeling invariable sites
    D. Incorporating codon models

27. The General Time Reversible (GTR) model includes:
    A. One rate parameter
    B. Six distinct substitution rates and base frequencies
    C. Only transition rates
    D. Codon position weighting

28. Among‐site rate heterogeneity is commonly modeled with:
    A. Uniform rates across sites
    B. A gamma distribution (+Γ)
    C. Only invariant sites (+I)
    D. Bootstrap resampling

29. In Maximum Likelihood, “likelihood” is defined as:
    A. P(data | model)
    B. P(model | data)
    C. P(tree)
    D. P(prior)

30. In Bayesian phylogenetics, the posterior probability P(model | data) is proportional to:
    A. Likelihood × Prior / Marginal likelihood
    B. Likelihood / Prior
    C. Prior / Likelihood
    D. Marginal likelihood × Prior

## Set 2 – Questions

1. In phylogenetic context, homology refers to:
   A. Similarity due to shared ancestry
   B. Similarity of function but not ancestry
   C. Similarity by chance
   D. Similarity due to selection pressures

2. Analogy in character analysis means:
   A. Common ancestry of structures
   B. Similar function without common ancestry
   C. Shared derived traits
   D. Same gene order

3. Which of the following is NOT a desirable criterion for a phylogenetic character?
   A. Homologous across taxa
   B. Heritable
   C. Independent
   D. Correlated with other characters

4. Homoplasy refers to:
   A. Similarity not due to common ancestry
   B. Similarity inherited from a common ancestor
   C. Gap penalties in alignment
   D. A measure of tree length

5. Which of these is an example of convergent evolution?
   A. Feathers in birds
   B. Camera‐type eyes in octopus and vertebrates
   C. Four-limbed tetrapods
   D. Vertebrae in mammals

6. The Neutral Theory implies:
   A. Most molecular changes are adaptive
   B. Many changes are neutral and fixed by drift
   C. Selection is the sole driver of evolution
   D. Morphological traits evolve neutrally

7. An orthogroup is:
   A. Pairs of orthologs between two species
   B. Genes derived from a single duplication event
   C. A set of orthologs descending from a common ancestor
   D. A cluster of paralogs

8. One‐to‐many orthologs arise when:
   A. Gene duplication occurs before speciation
   B. Duplication follows a speciation event in one lineage
   C. Genes lose function in one species
   D. HGT introduces extra copies

9. Out‐paralogs are paralogous genes that:
   A. Originate after a speciation event
   B. Originate within a lineage post-speciation
   C. Originate before a speciation event
   D. Are co-orthologs in many-to-many

10. In‐paralogs are paralogs that:
    A. Arise after a given speciation event
    B. Arise before speciation
    C. Derive from horizontal transfer
    D. Are one-to-one orthologs

11. A major limitation of RBH is that it:
    A. Fails to detect one‐to‐one orthologs
    B. Cannot handle genome synteny
    C. Only works for proteins
    D. Misses one-to-many or many-to-many orthologs

12. After identifying orthogroups, the next step in phylogenomic pipeline is:
    A. Tree reconciliation
    B. Sequence alignment
    C. Distance matrix calculation
    D. Partitioning analysis

13. The primary goal of sequence alignment is to:
    A. Identify orthologous positions with shared history
    B. Maximize overall percent identity
    C. Cluster sequences by length
    D. Annotate coding regions

14. Which tool is commonly used to trim poorly aligned regions?
    A. Gblocks
    B. ClustalW
    C. MAFFT
    D. BLAST

15. Needleman‐Wunsch algorithm is:
    A. A global alignment method
    B. A local alignment method
    C. A progressive multiple alignment
    D. A tree-building algorithm

16. Smith‐Waterman algorithm is:
    A. Global alignment
    B. Local alignment
    C. Phylogenetic tree construction
    D. Profile HMM search

17. An affine gap penalty is defined by:
    A. c = -d * g (linear)
    B. c = -d
    C. c = -d - (g - 1)e
    D. c = -(d + g*e)

18. Distance-based methods compute:
    A. Pairwise distances from alignments
    B. Character states per site
    C. Likelihood scores
    D. Posterior probabilities

19. A p-distance matrix uses:
    A. Raw proportion of differences
    B. Model‐corrected distances
    C. Weighted character changes
    D. Bayesian estimates

20. UPGMA uses:
    A. Average linkage clustering
    B. Minimum‐evolution criterion
    C. Maximum likelihood estimates
    D. Random joining

21. Neighbor‐Joining optimizes:
    A. Ultrametricity
    B. Minimum‐evolution criterion without clock
    C. Maximum parsimony
    D. Profile alignment

22. Parsimony informative sites must:
    A. Have at least two states each in ≥2 taxa
    B. Be invariant
    C. Include singleton states
    D. Be unique to one taxon

23. Parsimony tree length is the:
    A. Number of taxa
    B. Sum of character-state changes
    C. Total branch‐length units
    D. Model likelihood

24. Rate variation (+Γ) accounts for:
    A. Equal rates across sites
    B. Gamma-distributed site rates
    C. Invariant sites only
    D. Bootstrapping

25. Invariant sites (+I) model:
    A. Gamma-distribution of rates
    B. Codon-based substitution
    C. Explicit site profiles
    D. A proportion of sites unchanging

26. Substitution models are:
    A. Deterministic
    B. Markov chains with state transitions
    C. Parsimony criteria
    D. Alignment algorithms

27. GTR model is:
    A. Non-reversible
    B. Time-reversible
    C. Transition-only
    D. Codon-explicit

28. F81 model allows:
    A. Transition/transversion bias
    B. Unequal base frequencies
    C. Invariable sites
    D. Codon assignments

29. K80 model distinguishes:
    A. Codon changes
    B. Transitions vs transversions
    C. Rate heterogeneity
    D. Global vs local alignment

30. TN93 model includes:
    A. One rate for all changes
    B. Two distinct transition rates and one transversion rate
    C. Codon-based parameters

    A. Selection across all sites equally

    C. Neutral evolution only

    A. Closely related proteins (>85% identity)

    A. Using only mitochondrial proteins

    A. Gamma variation only
